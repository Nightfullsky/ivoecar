Aston Martin Valkyrie: What You Need to Know

By George Kennedy
|
June 23, 2022
|
By George Kennedy
|
June 23, 2022, at 4:36 p.m.
Credit

Aston Martin Lagonda Limited|

Pushing the Limits of the Automobile

What if you could design a passenger car where the only rules are for the race track? What would it look like? That is just the question that Aston Martin sought out to answer. Working in collaboration with Red Bull Racing, they created the insane Aston Martin Valkyrie hypercar.

Advertisement

The challenge was anything but simple: develop a car for road and track use. Such daily track cars exist, but the goal with the Valkyrie was to be fast enough to hang with actual race cars using inspiration and expertise from Formula 1 racing. There is even a version built to endurance racing specifications. Read on to learn more about this incredible automotive work of engineering.

Advertisement

Next:Development
Credit

Aston Martin Lagonda Limited|

Development

In its design and development phase, the Valkyrie was named AM-RB 001, indicating the partnership of Aston Martin and Red Bull. The latter is one of the top teams in Formula 1, and top personnel was involved, such as Red Bull's Chief Technical Officer Adrian Newey.

Advertisement

The two companies started from a clean sheet and tore up the rule book when it comes to designing cars. The result is a radical carbon fiber vehicle with specially designed tires and features you would sooner find on a dedicated race car. And yet, two of the three versions of this hypercar are street-legal.

Advertisement

Next:Price and Trims
Credit

Aston Martin Lagonda Limited|

Price and Trims

There’s an old adage with cars like this, “If you have to ask…” But if you must, there are three versions of the Valkyrie: the Coupe, AMR Pro and Spider. The Coupe is the “base” model, if you could even call it that. It has a starting price of $3 million. The Spider will set you back $4 million.

Advertisement

Splitting the difference in price ($3.5 million) is the AMR Pro. The “AMR” stands for Aston Martin Racing, and the AMR Pro is designed to deliver performance that could rival a 24 Hours of Le Mans prototype race car.

Advertisement

Next:Exterior Design
Credit

Aston Martin Lagonda Limited|

Exterior Design

The Valkyrie’s styling is informed by the wind tunnel. Downforce is essentially the way an airplane wing works, but is flipped over in order to suck the car to the ground. Incredible cornering and lateral grip are achieved by channeling air over, around and — in this case — through the car. In particular, the AMR Pro’s radical bodywork achieves 1000 kg (2,206 lbs.) of downforce. Looking at the Coupe from the rear, you can see the incredible space beneath the cockpit, designed to create Venturi tunnels.

Advertisement

The Coupe and Spider feature the required elements to make it a street-legal road car, including head-and-taillights, and a space for a rear license plate. The wheels are a single lug, designed to be removed quickly for speedy tire changes at a track event. The exhaust outlet on top in the rear, is similar to some other hypercars, as well as Formula 1 cars. And just like F1 cars, the high rear spoiler features a DRS-style (Drag Reduction System) rear spoiler, which can open the upper plane for higher speeds on the straights.

Advertisement

Next:Interior Design and Quality
Credit

Alexander Migl | Wikimedia Commons

Interior Design and Quality

It’s all about function in the Valkyrie, so stepping into the cabin is more like climbing into the cockpit of a fighter jet. With the gullwing doors lifted (butterfly doors on the Spider), there is still the tremendous carbon fiber chassis to climb over.

Advertisement

Once inside, it’s a snug two-passenger fit. Once inside, it’s a no-frills experience, as most of the interior is the exposed carbon fiber chassis. There are bits of Alcantara and other soft-touch materials for the dash, steering wheel and armrest, but don’t expect a lavish grand-touring car here.

Advertisement

Next:Interior Features
Credit

Aston Martin Lagonda Limited|

Interior Features

As the Valkyrie is inspired by race cars, the cabin is Aston Martin’s passenger-friendly take on a racing cockpit. In addition to a center screen, there is a screen built right into the steering wheel, and two digital displays at each corner of the dash. These corner screens replace conventional side mirrors, which aid in aerodynamics.

Advertisement

The fourth screen is the digital gauge cluster, which is located in the center of the steering wheel. This is a common design in Formula 1 cars, and like F1 cars, most of the Valkyrie’s driver controls are located on the rectangular steering wheel. And just like an F1 car, the steering wheel is removable for easier entry and egress.

Advertisement

Next:Seats
Credit

Aston Martin Lagonda Limited|

Seats

The Valkyrie features a pair of carbon-fiber bucket seats with five-point racing harnesses. They are fixed directly to the carbon fiber monocoque. Due to the tight spaces and need for utmost spacial efficiency, each seat is form-fit to the owner using 3-D scanning. As the seats are fixed, the steering wheel position and pedals are adjustable.

Advertisement

Considering this level of commitment to performance, it should come as a surprise to learn there is no front trunk, rear trunk or serious cargo space of any kind.

Advertisement

Next:Powertrain
Credit

Aston Martin Lagonda Limited|

Powertrain

The Valkyrie’s powertrain takes great inspiration from Formula 1, including the KERS (Kinetic Energy Recovery System) hybrid layout. But where the current Formula 1 cars use a small V6 engine (albeit with 1,000+ hp), the Valkyrie employs a 6.5-liter V12 engine that revs to a glorious 11,100 rpm. The engine is naturally aspirated (no turbo- or supercharging), and has been developed in partnership with Cosworth, a British race-engine outfit.

Advertisement

The electric end of this hybrid powertrain includes a 160-hp motor and a 40 kWh battery from Croatian electric hypercar maker Rimac. The whole thing makes a combined 1,160 horsepower and 664 pound-feet of torque. Power is routed to the rear wheels through a 7-speed single-clutch automated manual made by Ricardo.

Advertisement

Next:Acceleration
Credit

Aston Martin Lagonda Limited|

Acceleration

With all that power and a curb weight of just 2,271 pounds, the Valkyrie boasts a power-to-weight ratio of 1,126 per ton. The results are blisteringly quick acceleration and a zero-to-sixty time of just 2.6 seconds. The Valkyrie’s top speed is estimated at 250 mph, while the AMR Pro—which favors cornering prowess over top speed—can still hit 225 mph!

Advertisement

For the Spider, the top speed with the roof on is 217 mph. Even with the roof off, the Spider can hit 205 mph, making it the fastest-ever Aston Martin convertible.

Advertisement

Next:Ride and Handling
Credit

Aston Martin Lagonda Limited|

Ride and Handling

The Valkyrie features a special pushrod suspension, inspired by (you guessed it) Formula 1 cars. But Aston Martin took things a step further and provides an active suspension system — something that is banned in Formula 1. The active suspension works in tandem with the active aerodynamics, which includes four active control surfaces throughout the car.

Advertisement

The Valkyrie Coupe and Spider feature Michelin Pilot Sport Cup 2 tires, which are ultra-high-performance tires. For the AMR Pro, it developed special tires, which are based on Le Mans Prototype racing tires. The AMR Pro also features carbon-ceramic brakes.

Advertisement

Next:Spider and AMR Pro
Credit

Aston Martin Lagonda Limited|

Spider and AMR Pro

While the Coupe on its own is an incredible vehicle, the Spider and AMR Pro have some features that set it apart. As mentioned, the Spider has butterfly-style doors as it does not have a roof. In fact, the roof section, which has its own small, glass gullwing hatches, cannot be stowed in the car. It’ll have to be stowed in one of the owner’s six garage bays.

Advertisement

The AMR Pro features reworked aerodynamics to create insane amounts of downforce. Like an LMP-1 race car, it features a dorsal fin for added stability, conventional side mirrors and a massive rear wing that runs from fender to fender in a big, undulating arc. Luxury items such as air conditioning and the infotainment system have been removed.

Advertisement

Next:Safety Features
Credit

Aston Martin Lagonda Limited|

Safety Features

You won’t find your usual suite of modern driver assistance tech in the Valkyrie. Much of the safety is provided by the extremely strong carbon fiber structure and five-point racing harnesses. In the Valkyrie, traction control and electronic stability control count as advanced safety tech.

Advertisement

Advertisement

Next:The Competition
Credit

Mercedes-Benz Group AG|

The Competition

This hypercar plays in rarified air, but there are some other contenders for the “What If?” category of race-cars-for-the-road. The most notable is the Mercedes-AMG ONE, which features a Formula 1-inspired drivetrain (hence the name). It features a twin-turbo 1.6-liter V6 in a hybrid setup that makes 1,000 combined horsepower.

Advertisement

This powertrain and the lightweight chassis allow the ONE to accelerate from zero to 124 mph in under six seconds and climb to a top speed in excess of 217 mph. It was announced in 2017 and is expected to arrive this year. All 275 units have been spoken for, with a starting price of approximately $2.75 million.

The ONE and AMR Pro share one disappointing detail — they are both what remains of each company’s scuttled attempts to enter the 12 Hours of Le Mans. And both are pretty incredible consolation prizes.

Advertisement

Next:Availability and Production
Credit

Aston Martin Lagonda Limited|

Availability and Production

The first Valkyrie Coupe was delivered to a customer in November of 2021. Each hypercar is hand-assembled over the course of 2,000 man-hours at Aston Martin’s Gaydon, UK headquarters.

Advertisement

150 examples of the Coupe will be built, and the Spider will be limited to just 85 units. The rarest of them all, unsurprisingly, is the AMR Pro. Just 40 examples of the track-only machine will be assembled.
